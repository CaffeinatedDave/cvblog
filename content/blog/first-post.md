I once moved an engineer out of my team because I was told he was disruptive.

That description wasn’t entirely unfair. He was vocal and frequently challenging. He did not always express himself particularly carefully. I spent months dealing with complaints about his attitude and defending his place within the team. Eventually, I lost the argument with my own manager and moved him elsewhere.

I thought I was resolving a difficult situation. The rest of the team saw me removing someone who cared deeply about them and the work they were doing.

They revolted.

The decision was swiftly reversed, and the supposedly problematic engineer was later promoted (and did very well by all accounts). His approach could be difficult, but the disruption came from a genuine determination to make the team better. I had spent too much time firefighting complaints about how he communicated and not enough time explaining the value behind what he was saying.

I still take the outcome personally. I should have protected the culture of the team more strongly, even when doing so made conversations outside the team uncomfortable. That does not mean ignoring bad behaviour because somebody produces good work or has good intentions. It means separating the challenge someone creates inside a team from the discomfort they create for people outside it.

My approach now is to protect people from external pressure while challenging them directly and honestly within the team. That balance is rarely straightforward. Sometimes outside criticism is justified, and sometimes disruption really is damaging the team. But discomfort is not proof of damage. 

The difficult person in the room may simply be the one that still cares enough to say what everyone else has stopped saying.
